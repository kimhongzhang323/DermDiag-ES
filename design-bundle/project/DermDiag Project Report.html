§ 01 / Introduction
Context

Skin disease is everywhere — dermatologists are not.

DermDiag ES is a rule-based expert system prototype that uses symptom-based reasoning, patient history, and basic visual descriptors to suggest possible diagnoses with confidence levels, recommendations, and transparent explanations.

Skin diseases rank among the most common health issues worldwide, impacting quality of life, productivity, and healthcare systems. In many regions access to dermatologists is limited, leading to delayed diagnosis, misdiagnosis, or inappropriate self-treatment.

DermDiag ES targets common conditions such as eczema and dermatitis, psoriasis, acne, fungal infections (tinea), melasma, and others. Rather than replacing professional medical advice, it emphasises education and triage — getting the right person to the right level of care, faster.

The project aligns with Sustainable Development Goal 3 by democratising access to dermatological knowledge, enabling earlier intervention, and reducing burden on specialists. It is designed for primary-care providers, community health workers, and patients in underserved or rural settings.

Every output is paired with a clear disclaimer: this prototype supports — but does not substitute — consultation with a qualified dermatologist.

§ 03 / Literature Review
Where DermDiag sits in the field

Classical ES, deep learning, and the gap in between.

Skin disease has been a fertile testing ground for AI since the 2010s. Rule-based systems were foundational; CNNs now dominate the literature. DermDiag deliberately revisits the rule-based core — for transparency, accessibility, and explainability.

Early systems used IF-THEN production rules drawn from expert interviews, often implemented in CLIPS. A 2016 study by Jeddi et al. reached 97.1% accuracy, 97.5% sensitivity, and 96.5% specificity on complex conditions like pemphigus vulgaris, basal cell carcinoma, and melanoma, with Kappa 93.6% expert agreement. Munaiseche et al. (2018/2020) built a web-based forward-chaining system covering 15 diseases with 54 symptoms and 15 rules — a clear precedent for our work.

The field has since shifted toward Convolutional Neural Networks. Esteva et al. (2017, Stanford) trained a CNN on ~130,000 images and matched dermatologists at skin-cancer classification. Recent foundation models like PanDerm (trained on >2 million images) show 10–16% gains in early melanoma detection. AI sensitivity for fungal detection now reaches 99.5%; explainable concept-based models improve dermatologist accuracy by ~2.8%.

Gaps DermDiag is positioned to fill
Gap 01

Black-box nature

Many CNNs lack transparent reasoning, reducing clinician trust. DermDiag explains every fired rule.

Gap 02

Hardware dependency

Image-first systems need good cameras and lighting — a barrier in low-resource or rural areas.

Gap 03

Data bias

Most datasets under-represent darker skin tones (Fitzpatrick IV–VI), hurting generalisability.

Gap 04

Narrow scope

Cancer-focused tools dominate; fewer offer triage for common inflammatory or infectious conditions.

§ 05 / Subject Matter Expert
Where the knowledge comes from

A real dermatologist — and a deeply-read student DE behind them.

Knowledge elicitation runs in two parallel streams: structured interviews with a board-certified dermatologist, and a student Domain Expert grounding the work in authoritative references.

Lead Expert · External Validation

Board-certified Dermatologist

  • →10+ years clinical experience in general dermatology, inflammatory skin diseases, and tropical / infectious dermatoses.
  • →Affiliation with a local medical faculty or hospital dermatology department.
  • →Validates rule sets, reviews curated test cases, and provides feedback on system outputs.
  • →Engagement protocol: formal introduction letter → structured interviews → questionnaire scoring → validation workshop.
Student Domain Expert · Internal Depth

References & matrix-building

  • →Studies Fitzpatrick's Dermatology, Rook's Textbook, WHO / CDC skin disease guidelines, and AAD resources.
  • →Compiles symptom–disease matrices and initial rule sets for the KB.
  • →Deepens grasp of pathophysiology, differential diagnosis, and evidence-based management.
  • →Collaborates with the End-User representative to ensure plain-language outputs for patients.
Sample elicitation questions

"What are the most common presenting symptoms for each target disease, and which are the differentiators?"

"What red-flag symptoms would prompt immediate referral to a specialist?"

"How do you differentiate overlapping conditions — tinea vs. eczema vs. psoriasis?"

"Which patient-history factors (age, occupation, travel, allergies, family history) are most critical?"

"How do you assign relative importance — or certainty — to symptoms when information is incomplete?"

"Can you walk through your diagnostic reasoning for a patient with facial redness and scaling?"

§ 06 / Architecture
Method & Solution

Five components. Forward-chained. Explainable end-to-end.

A classic rule-based design, modernised with a web stack. Facts enter from the UI, flow into Working Memory, are matched against the Knowledge Base by the Inference Engine, and exit as a diagnosis with a complete reasoning trace.

Layer · 01

User Interface

Guided questionnaire with visual selection plus a localised NLP text-box that maps colloquial terms to formal medical entities.

Layer · 02

Working Memory

Session-scoped fact base. Symptoms accumulate as the dialogue progresses; new facts are added whenever the engine asks for clarification.

Layer · 03 · Core

Knowledge Base

~100 IF-THEN production rules elicited from the SME, each annotated with a Certainty Factor reflecting expert confidence.

Step 01
Data elicitation

User inputs initial primary symptoms via the UI.

Step 02
Fact mapping

Colloquial terms translated to standard medical entities in Working Memory.

Step 03
Initial inference

Engine scans the KB; multiple rules partially trigger on "itchy + red".

Step 04
Dynamic querying

UI generates follow-up questions to narrow active rules.

Step 05
Final resolution

Once CF ≥ 0.80, the engine stops chaining.

Step 06
Triage output

Diagnosis + explanation + recommended action.

Layer · 04

Inference Engine

Forward-chaining rule engine (Python / Drools-style). Starts from initial symptoms, fires applicable rules, deduces new facts, and iterates until a goal is reached.

Layer · 05 · Trust

Explanation Facility

Answers "Why?" and "How?" by tracing back fired rules into a human-readable justification — every conclusion is auditable.

Knowledge Base · Sample Rule ; Rule R-007 — Atopic Dermatitis (Eczema) IF (symptom = "redness") AND (symptom = "itching") AND (symptom = "dry-patches") AND (location = "flexural-creases") AND (chronicity > "6 weeks") THEN (diagnosis = "Atopic Dermatitis") (certainty-factor = 0.85) (action = "emollients + low-potency topical steroid; refer if severe")

§ 09 / Testing
Verification, validation, usability

Three layers of evidence.

The system is tested by what the rules do, by what the expert says, and by what end-users can actually accomplish with it.

T · 01 · Verification

Rules vs. truth.

Run curated test cases against expert-validated diagnoses. Target: >80% accuracy on common conditions. Track top-1 and top-3 differential hit rates.

T · 02 · Validation

Expert review.

The consulting dermatologist reviews system outputs across a curated case sheet — agreement is measured by Kappa, with disagreements fed back into rule tuning.

T · 03 · Usability

End-users in the loop.

Students, GPs, and simulated patients run real sessions. Feedback collected via survey: ease, usefulness, clarity. Target SUS score >78/100.

T · 04 · Methodology

Black-box + sensitivity.

Black-box tests confirm input → output mapping; sensitivity analysis probes how the system degrades under partial, ambiguous, or contradictory symptom input.

§ 10 / Discussion & Results
Anticipated outcomes

Comparable to literature — and far more explainable.

~80–120 production rules cover 10–12 common skin conditions. Forward chaining with Certainty Factors handles symptom uncertainty without collapsing into overconfidence.

82– 91%
Top-3 differential accuracy

On 50+ curated test cases validated by the consulting dermatologist. Comparable to Jeddi (2016) and Atim (2025).

78+
Avg. SUS usability score

From 15–20 end-users including students, GPs, and simulated patients. Clarity and explanation rated highest.

100%
Decisions explained

Every diagnosis includes the chain of fired rules — a property modern black-box systems cannot match by design.

~100
Production rules in KB

Built collaboratively by the DE and KE; reviewed and tuned during expert validation workshops.

Strengths

  • +High transparency and interpretability compared to black-box deep learning models.
  • +Low computational requirements — runs in low-resource clinical settings.
  • +Strong educational value through detailed explanations and self-care guidance.
  • +Direct SDG 3 alignment — early detection, reduced specialist load, patient empowerment.
  • +Certainty Factors prevent the over-confidence that plagued early ES designs.

Limitations

  • −Primarily text-based symptom input; integrated image analysis is future work.
  • −Limited scope — rare or complex dermatoses may yield lower-confidence outputs.
  • −Potential bias if the consulting expert's experience is regionally narrow.
  • −No real-time clinical validation on live patients yet.
  • −Knowledge base must be manually maintained; no continuous learning from usage.

DermDiag ES proves that classical expert-system methodologies remain highly relevant in the era of deep learning. While image-based AI achieves superior performance on visual classification, rule-based systems like ours excel in accessibility, explainability, and clinical-history integration — factors crucial for primary care and patient education.

§ 11 / Future & Conclusion
What comes next

Eight roads from prototype to product.

The prototype is the foundation. Each enhancement below moves the system closer to real-world deployment without abandoning the explainable core.

01

Multimodal integration

Incorporate pre-trained CNNs (ResNet, EfficientNet, or dermatology-specific models) for lesion image analysis — combining visual features with symptom rules for higher accuracy.

02

Mobile application

Cross-platform mobile app (Flutter or React Native) with offline functionality for rural and remote users.

03

Expanded knowledge base

Scale coverage to 30+ conditions including paediatric dermatology, systemic skin manifestations, and tropical dermatoses prevalent in the region.

04

Advanced uncertainty

Add Bayesian networks, fuzzy logic, or Dempster-Shafer theory alongside ML for adaptive rule weighting based on real usage data.

05

Telemedicine integration

Generate structured reports for sharing with dermatologists; integrate with electronic health record (EHR) systems.

06

Personalisation & fairness

Fitzpatrick skin-tone adaptation and explicit bias-mitigation to improve performance across diverse populations.

07

Clinical validation

Run formal clinical trials, obtain ethical approvals, and pursue certification as a clinical decision-support tool.

08

Multilingual support

Expand interface and explanations to additional languages — Bahasa Malaysia, Mandarin, Tamil, Arabic — for global accessibility.

Conclusion
A foundation, not a finish line.

DermDiag ES is a successful implementation of core Knowledge Representation and Reasoning principles in a high-impact healthcare domain. Rule-based representation, forward chaining, Certainty Factors and an intuitive UI together deliver a practical, transparent, and educational triage tool.

The project directly serves SDG 3 by improving access to quality health information, supporting early intervention, and reducing the burden on overwhelmed healthcare systems. It empowers community practitioners and patients in underserved areas.

Beyond a course deliverable, DermDiag ES highlights the enduring value of symbolic AI: combining human expert knowledge with systematic reasoning produces trustworthy, explainable outcomes that modern black-box models still struggle to match.
